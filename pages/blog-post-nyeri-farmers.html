Meet the Farmers Behind Our Nyeri AA Beans

We travelled to the Central Highlands to visit the smallholder farms that supply our most popular single origin.

Every bag of Nyeri AA on our shelf can be traced back to one of eleven smallholder farms scattered across the ridges above the Aberdares. We spent three days there, and the thing that struck us most wasn't the scenery — it was how much care goes into a coffee cherry before it ever reaches a cup.

Why altitude matters

Most of our partner farms sit between 1,700 and 1,900 metres above sea level. At that altitude, cherries ripen more slowly, which concentrates sugars and acidity — it's the single biggest reason Kenyan AA grades taste the way they do: bright, winey, almost blackcurrant-like.

The hand-picking process

Pickers walk the same rows multiple times across a season, taking only fully ripe red cherries and leaving the rest for the next pass. It's slower and more expensive than stripping a branch all at once, but it's the only way to avoid unripe or overripe cherries diluting the final cup quality.

What "above fair-trade" actually means here

We pay a minimum of 30% above the Nairobi Coffee Exchange floor price directly to the farms, bypassing several layers of middlemen that usually eat into a farmer's margin. It means a slightly higher price for you, and a meaningfully better one for the people growing it.
