Latte Art for Beginners: Three Patterns to Start With

Brian walks through the heart, rosetta, and tulip — and the one milk-texturing mistake that ruins all three.

Every latte art pattern starts the same way: with milk that's been textured correctly. Get that wrong, and no amount of wrist control will save the pour.

The milk-texturing mistake almost everyone makes

Most beginners introduce air too late, after the milk has already started swirling. By then you get big, uneven bubbles instead of the fine, glossy microfoam you actually want. Aim to introduce a light hissing sound for the first 2-3 seconds only, then submerge the steam wand to build a smooth whirlpool.

The heart

The easiest pattern to start with. Pour from height into the centre of the cup until it's about two-thirds full, then drop the jug close to the surface and pour a steady stream straight through the middle, finishing with a quick flick forward.

The rosetta

Once you've got the heart down, the rosetta just adds a gentle side-to-side wiggle of the jug as you pour, walking backward across the cup before finishing with the same forward flick.

The tulip

The hardest of the three — it's really several small hearts stacked on top of each other. Pour a small heart, pause and push the cup slightly forward, then pour another on top. Repeat two or three times before finishing with the line through the middle.
